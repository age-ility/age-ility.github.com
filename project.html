The AGE-ility Project: Flexible Minds for Life

Seemingly simple routine behaviours actually involve the careful coordination, monitoring and adjustment of many underlying processes.

Consider, for example, the act of driving to work each day. You need to monitor traffic and adjust your speed accordingly, look out for pedestrians, check traffic lights at intersections, prepare to change lanes to take a turn, while maintaining a mental roadmap of the remaining route to your destination. Each subcomponent of driving requires the application and integration of many adaptive control processes. Yet, an experienced driver can effortlessly integrate all these processes into what appears to be a single task (i.e., driving to work) and still have residual capacity to carry out an intelligent conversation with fellow passengers.

However, this fine balance is easily tipped by an unexpected change in road conditions (e.g., an ambulance siren, a child running onto the road). This shifts adaptive cognitive processes from a supervisory mode to a full control mode, requiring the re-evaluation of immediate goals and adjustment of behaviour so as to prioritise safety. This full control mode is highly effortful, it needs our full attention and, as a result, other non-essential functions (i.e., talking, listening) are interrupted until the situation is cleared and a supervisory mode can be reinstated.

Consider now the striking contrast of a learner driver. The learner needs to concentrate on each component of the task, making driving effortful and disjointed, and any additional distraction (conversation, music) can be disruptive. In the learner driver, we can see that well-learned actions, such as driving, are composed of many subcomponents. Each of these require a different degree of cognitive control depending on internal (e.g., fatigue, practice) and external (e.g., traffic density, unexpected events) contingencies.

The Age-ility project focuses on one adaptive cognitive control process that is crucial to both safely negotiating routine actions, such as driving to work each day, and adapting to new or unexpected situations. Mental flexibility is the ability to strategically adjust behaviour so as to maximise positive outcomes in an “uncertain, unpredictable world”. Like other adaptive control processes, cognitive flexibility processes vary across the lifespan, peaking in mid-late 20s and declining in senescence. However, we know little about the factors that underlie this variability (i.e., changes in strategy, brain structure or both). This is the focus of this research.

Successful adaptation in our complex and unpredictable world depends on our ability to adapt to change by flexibly adjusting our behaviour. Mental flexibility and the brain networks that support it vary across the lifespan.

Building flexible minds

Most of us reach our peak in our mid-late 20s but the rate of maturation differs vastly across people. This rate depends on a complex interaction between our ‘hardware’ (for example, the genes we inherit from our parents) and our ‘software’ (how we use our life’s experiences to grow our brain). Healthy development into adulthood relies on learning how to deal with the challenges that arise at each stage of life and applying these skills to deal with bigger challenges in the future. Mental health problems can arise from maladaptive learning patterns through childhood and into young adulthood.

The Age-ility Project seeks to understand how individual differences in cognitive flexibility influence successful adaptation during the shift from adolescence to young adulthood. This is a critical period that often sets pathways for lifelong patterns of behaviour. It is important to understand the factors that influence successful adaptation and promote flexible behaviour. This information can help us identify patterns of problem behaviours early in life and develop age-specific interventions to promote adaptive outcomes and thereby promote a healthy start to life.

Flexible minds for life

The ability to flexibly adapt to new or challenging situations tends to decline in old age. But, there is large individual variability in the rate of decline. Indeed, many people retain highly efficient cognitive functioning well into old age.

What factors intensify the rate of decline in mental flexibility?

In this project, we examine the relationship between cognitive flexibility and changes in brain connectivity across the adult lifespan. Difficulties with adaptive control can compromise the ability to maintain an independent lifestyle, by interfering with activities of daily living.

Although there is a lot of interest in cognitive remediation in ageing, there has been little evidence of skill transfer into daily life. Using basic science to understand the mechanisms that mediate adaptive control difficulties in ageing can inform evidence-based remedial interventions that transfer into daily activities. In the long-term, this can help prolong healthy, independent ageing, which would have personal, social and economic implications for ageing well and productively.

In the long term, this information can be implemented to guide the design of targeted education/prevention programs which will promote development of adaptive behaviours and improve the transition from adolescence to adulthood and adulthood to senescence. By defining the factors that determine variation in adaptive executive control in a normative sample, we will have a firm baseline for understanding variability due to neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative disorders, such as schizophrenia, dementia, ADHD and other disorders. In this way, this knowledge will contribute to promotion of preventative healthcare.
