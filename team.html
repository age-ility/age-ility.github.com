The AGE-ility Project Team

Associate Professor Frini Karayanidis leads an international team of researchers in a longitudinal project that aspires to map the development of mental flexibility across the adult lifespan. This multi-disciplinary research team includes specialised skills in areas including child development, ageing, experimental psychology, clinical psychology, neurology, psychiatry, neuroimaging and psychophysiology.

The first phase of the Age-ility Project is funded by an ARC-Discovery Project grant. Funding for longitudinal components is currently being sought. This will allow us to follow up our cohort and examine implications of individual differences in mental flexibility for healthy, resilient development, promoting and maintaining good health in old age and preventative mental health.

Associate Professor Frini Karayanidis

School of Psychology, Functional Neuroimaging Laboratory, University of Newcastle

Convenor, Development, Ageing and Cognition Program, Priority Research Centre for Translational Neuroscience & Mental Health

Steering committee member, Adolescents, Babies and Children (ABC) Research Program, Hunter Medical Research Institute (HMRI)

http://www.newcastle.edu.au/staff/profile/frini.karayanidis.html

A/Professor Karayanidis runs an integrated and collaborative research program that aims to understand the organisation of complex cognitive processes, such as mental flexibility. The aim of this work is to help us understand individual differences in cognitive control and how they influence successful adaptive functioning, e.g., emotional control, social integration? This program is couched within a model of lifespan development that advocates the need for integrative methodologies to understand continuity in development across the lifespan by examining links between cognitive, neuronal and behavioural levels of analysis.

Emeritus Professor Pat Michie

School of Psychology, Functional Neuroimaging Laboratory, University of Newcastle

http://www.newcastle.edu.au/staff/research-profile/Pat_Michie/

E/Professor Michie has held professorial positions at the University of Newcastle, University of Western Australia and Macquarie University. E/Prof Michie's primary research interest is in the area of cognitive neuroscience, particularly the neural basis of auditory information processing, attention and executive functions in healthy individuals, in people with schizophrenia and those at risk of psychosis. She has on-going research collaborators will colleagues in Finland, Japan, Perth, Sydney and Brisbane. E/Prof Michie has been awarded more than $5M in NHMRC funds over the period from 2001-2014. She has served as Board member of the Schizophrenia Research Institute and represented the University of Newcastle at a senior executive level as Pro-Vice Chancellor Research and Acting Deputy Vice Chancellor Research.

Professor Dr Birte Forstmann

Cognitive Science Center Amsterdam (CSCA), School of Psychology, University of Amsterdam

http://www.birteforstmann.com/

Professor Forstmann’s research goal is to understand the brain mechanisms that allow people to adapt quickly to changes in their environment. Her work combines mathematical modeling with functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), ultra-high resolution 7T MRI, and electroencephalography (EEG). Her research capitalizes on individual differences in psychological processes involved in decision-making and interference control. Dr Forstmann has a long-standing collaboration with the School of Psychology where she spends part of her year.

Professor Rhoshel Lenroot

Chair of Infant, Child, and Adolescent Psychiatry School of Psychiatry, University of New South Wales

Neuroscience Research Australia, NSW

Clinical Director of Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services, Southeast and Illawarra Area Health Service

http://www.neura.edu.au/research/people/profiles/dr-rhoshel-lenroot

Professor Lenroot received her medical degree and training in Adult, Child, and Adolescent Psychiatry at the University of New Mexico in the United States, where she undertook neuroimaging to study adolescents with early onset schizophrenia. In Bethesda, Maryland, she worked on longitudinal studies of brain development with the Child Psychiatry Branch of the National Institutes of Mental Health. Areas of interest included sex differences, twin studies to parse out the relative of influences of genetic and environmental factor across development, and disorders such as autism. Her current work is focused on neuroimaging studies of brain development, how the development of social cognition is disturbed in disorders such as autism and schizophrenia, and improving mental health interventions for children and adolescents.

Associate Professor Mark Parsons

Staff Specialist in Neurology at the John Hunter Hospital

ARC Future Fellow, University of Newcastle

Convenor, Stroke Research Program, Priority Research Centre for Translational Neuroscience & Mental Health

http://www.newcastle.edu.au//staff/research-profile/Mark_Parsons/

A/Professor Parsons was Director of the Calvary Mater Hospital Stroke Unit from 2003 to 2009 and established the Imaging Research Laboratory at John Hunter Hospital. His brain imaging research has been adopted in clinical practice to improve the selection of patients for acute stroke therapies involving clot-dissolving treatments which can reduce a stroke patient’s level of disability and lead to a quicker recovery when administered immediately after a stroke. His research interests also include the adverse effects of diabetes on stroke outcomes and neurological rehabilitation and recovery. His findings linking hyperglycaemia and adverse stroke outcome have had major implications for research in this field and potentially, clinical guidelines in the future.

Professor Natalie Phillips

Department of Psychology, Concordia University

Centre for Research in Human Development

Centre for Research on Language Mind and Brain

Department Clinical Neurosciences, Jewish General Hospital

http://psychology.concordia.ca/fac/phillips/

Professor Phillips’ area of research expertise concerns the neuropsychology of executive function in health young and older adults and in patients with or at risk for Alzheimer disease or other dementias. She is interested in identifying factors which help or hinder our understanding of language and how these are influenced by age. Current research examines age differences in audio-visual speech processing. With respect to Alzheimer's disease, Dr. Phillips is attempting to identify electrophysiological (ERP) markers of brain dysfunction that will lead to improved early diagnosis. Her team has developed and validated the MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment), a brief cognitive screening test used in the detection of Mild Cognitive Impairment in the elderly.

Professor E.J. Wagenmakers

Professor of Neurocognitive Modeling,
Faculty of Social and Behavioural Sciences of the University of Amsterdam (UvA).

http://www.ejwagenmakers.com/

Professor Wagenmakers’ research focuses on developing and testing mathematical models for cognition. Such models enable researchers to bridge the gap between data that can be directly measured (e.g., response times, percentage correct decisions, neuronal firing frequencies) and the latent cognitive mechanisms upon which these data are based (e.g., processing speed, caution, sensitivity to reward, and control processes). As part of his appointment, Wagenmakers will promote a further integration of different disciplines - genetics, neuroscience, psychology, biology, physics, economics, linguistics – under the theme of 'cognition'. An essential element of this integration will be the focus on formal models for behaviour, cognition, and information processing.

Mr Patrick Cooper

PhD Student
School of Psychology, Functional Neuroimaging Laboratory, University of Newcastle

Mr Cooper completed his Bachelor of Psychology (Honours I) in 2011 under the supervision of A/Prof Karayanidis. He has received extensive training in ERP and MRI data acquisition and processing, including a training course on functional connectivity at Martinos Center for Biomedical Imaging at Harvard University. His thesis will focus on using complex network models of cognitive control to investigate the relationship between individual differences in neural network architecture and cognitive flexibility.
